How I started acting differently:
I definitely became more aware of how I react to annoying situations. Before this week, I probably would have just complained right away or gotten visibly frustrated. But I started catching myself and choosing to take deep breaths instead. I also found myself trying to encourage my groupmates when they got stuck with coding problems, instead of adding to the stress by being grumpy too. I got better at arriving early and just accepting that waiting is part of life, especially with my classmates who are always late. I stopped expecting them to be on time and just naturally wait for them.

How my thinking changed:
The biggest change was realizing that staying patient actually made me better at solving problems. When I was calm, I could think more clearly about coding issues and figure out solutions faster than when I was stressed out and rushing. I started seeing problems like bugs and delays as just normal parts of life instead of personal attacks on my day.
I also accepted that some things are just out of my control, like the weather, power outages, internet problems, and other people being late. Getting worked up about these things doesn't change anything, so why waste my energy being mad?

Even though I didn't write about it much in my daily entries, I think my groupmates appreciated that I stayed calm during our coding sessions. When I wasn't getting frustrated and complaining, it seemed like everyone could focus better and we worked together more smoothly. The atmosphere felt less tense when I kept my cool. My classmates probably noticed that I didn't complain as much about waiting, even when they were really late. I think they felt less guilty because I wasn't making them feel bad about it.
What I learned overall:
By the end of the week, being patient felt a little more natural, though I still had to really try hard sometimes. The most important thing I realized is that patience isn't just about suffering through bad situations quietly, it's about staying calm so you can think better and handle things more effectively. I'm still not perfect at it, and I probably still have moments where I almost break down, but at least now I know that patience actually works and makes things better, not worse.

Virtue Tracker Challenge: Build Your Character One Day at a Time!

Objective: Encourage the development of moral virtues through conscious, daily practice. By choosing one specific virtue to focus on, you will gain insight into your behavior, values, and the challenges and rewards of living virtuously.
1
Choose Your Virtue
Start by selecting one moral virtue that you would like to strengthen over the next 7 days. Examples of virtues include:
  • Honesty - Telling the truth, even when it's hard
  • Kindness - Being compassionate and helping others
  • Courage - Doing the right thing despite fear
  • Patience - Staying calm in frustrating situations
2
Create Your Virtue Tracker
Design a simple chart or journal page where you will record your daily efforts. Your Virtue Tracker should include:
  • Date
  • Action Taken (What did you do to practice the virtue?)
  • Situation or Context
  • Reflection (How did it feel? What was challenging or rewarding?)
3
Practice Your Virtue Daily
Each day, make a conscious effort to perform at least one action that reflects your chosen virtue. This could be:
  • Helping a classmate (kindness)
  • Owning up to a mistake (honesty)
  • Staying calm when provoked (patience)
It's okay if the action is small—what matters is your intention and awareness!
4
Track and Reflect
Every evening (or right after the act), fill in your tracker. Think about:
  • How did practicing this virtue make you feel?
  • Was it easier or harder than you expected?
  • What did you learn from the experience?
5
End-of-Week Reflection
After 7 days, take time to reflect on your experience by answering the following two questions in a short-written reflection or discussion:
  1. What was easy or difficult about practicing your chosen virtue?
  2. Did you notice any changes in your behavior, mindset, or how others reacted to you?
